This will help a lot in teaching them best practices (and learn to appreciate the skill we bring to the table). We found that two major topics are missing from your list, namely plurals and (grammatical) gender. 

1) Misconception: Every language has very simple plural forms.

Very often, English natives tend to think that you can just append an "s" to a word for plurals, even though that doesn't even properly work in English. Most other languages have a plethora of irregular plural forms that need to be accounted for, some languages even need to have a four-way distinction between "0", "1", "few", and "many" things. (The "a/an" distinction is another thing that seems ridiculously simple in English and causes problems in many other languages if it's coded as a separate string.)

2) Misconception: Like English, most languages don't use grammatical gender. Like English, the gender of the speakers and addressees doesn't matter in most cases.

Grammatical gender (and character gender in case of video games) is another topic that always leads to headaches. The English language has that handy trick of being able to use "they" and "them" in case a characters gender isn't known or should be concealed. That doesn't work in many other languages. In languages like French, e.g., adjectives will need to be adjusted depending on the speaker's and/or addressee's gender. Something similar happens with pronouns. The possessive in German is both dependent on the subjects gender and the objects grammatical gender (his father: sein Vater, his mother: seine Mutter, her father: ihr Vater, her mother: ihre Mutter).

Numbers written out as digits:
Indian languages use the lakh/crore system instead of millions/billions, so the commas are in different places.

1,00,00,000.00

Ordinals: French does use letters after the digit, but they represent the French words, so 1er, 2ème, 3ème, etc.

Another fun one is whether the currency symbol goes before or after the digits, depends on the language, so you'll see both €100 and 100€ depending where you are in Europe.
